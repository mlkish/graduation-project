Caffeine: Pharmacology, Effects, and Sources

Caffeine is the world's most widely consumed psychoactive substance. A naturally occurring stimulant of the methylxanthine class, it is found in the seeds, leaves, and fruits of more than 60 plant species, where it acts as a natural pesticide. Chemically it is 1,3,7-trimethylxanthine. Unlike most psychoactive drugs, it is legal, unregulated in most contexts, and integrated into daily life through coffee, tea, and soft drinks.

How caffeine works

Caffeine's primary mechanism is antagonism of adenosine receptors in the brain. Adenosine is a neuromodulator that accumulates during waking hours and promotes drowsiness by binding to its receptors and slowing neural activity. Caffeine is structurally similar to adenosine and binds to the same receptors without activating them, thereby blocking adenosine's sedating signal. The result is increased neuronal firing and the release of stimulating neurotransmitters such as dopamine and norepinephrine. This is why caffeine reduces the perception of fatigue and increases alertness — it does not add energy so much as mask sleepiness.

Pharmacokinetics

Caffeine is absorbed rapidly and almost completely from the gut, reaching peak blood levels roughly 30–60 minutes after consumption. It is highly bioavailable and crosses the blood–brain barrier easily. Its half-life — the time for the body to eliminate half a dose — averages about 3–5 hours in healthy adults but varies widely, from around 2 hours to over 10, depending on genetics, liver enzyme activity (chiefly the CYP1A2 enzyme), pregnancy (which markedly slows clearance), and other factors such as smoking (which speeds it up). This variability is why caffeine consumed in the afternoon can still disrupt sleep at night for slow metabolizers.

Effects on the body and mind

At moderate doses (roughly 40–300 mg), caffeine can improve alertness, attention, reaction time, mood, and endurance during physical exertion. It modestly raises heart rate and blood pressure, stimulates stomach acid production, and has a mild diuretic effect. In athletes it is a well-documented ergogenic aid, improving both endurance and, to a lesser degree, high-intensity performance.

At higher doses, side effects include anxiety, jitteriness, restlessness, insomnia, rapid heartbeat (palpitations), and gastrointestinal upset. People vary greatly in sensitivity. Health authorities such as the U.S. FDA and the European Food Safety Authority generally consider up to about 400 mg per day (roughly four cups of brewed coffee) safe for most healthy adults, with lower limits advised during pregnancy (typically under 200 mg per day). Caffeine can be dangerous in very large amounts; lethal doses are estimated at roughly 10 grams or more for adults, a level essentially impossible to reach through coffee but achievable with concentrated powders or pills.

Tolerance, dependence, and withdrawal

Regular use leads to tolerance: the brain increases the number of adenosine receptors, so more caffeine is needed for the same effect. Physical dependence develops, and abruptly stopping produces a genuine withdrawal syndrome — headache, fatigue, difficulty concentrating, irritability, and low mood — typically beginning 12–24 hours after the last dose, peaking within a day or two, and resolving within about a week. Because of this, much of caffeine's perceived "boost" for habitual drinkers may partly be the relief of withdrawal rather than enhancement above baseline.

Sources and caffeine content

Approximate caffeine content:
- Brewed coffee (240 ml cup): about 80–100 mg, though it ranges widely.
- Espresso (single shot, ~30 ml): about 60–75 mg.
- Black tea: about 40–70 mg per cup; green tea somewhat less.
- Cola soft drinks: about 30–40 mg per can.
- Energy drinks: highly variable, often 80–160 mg per can.
- Dark chocolate: roughly 12–25 mg per ounce.
- Decaffeinated coffee still contains a small amount, typically 2–5 mg per cup.

Health research

Long-term observational studies associate moderate coffee consumption with no net harm and possible modest benefits, including lower risks of type 2 diabetes, Parkinson's disease, and certain liver conditions — though these are correlations, not proven cause and effect, and coffee contains many bioactive compounds besides caffeine. Overall, current evidence supports moderate caffeine intake as safe and potentially beneficial for most healthy adults, while individual sensitivity, pregnancy, certain heart conditions, and anxiety disorders warrant more caution.
