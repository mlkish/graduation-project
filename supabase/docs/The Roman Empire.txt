The Roman Empire: Rise, Structure, and Fall

Rome grew from a small settlement on the Tiber River, traditionally founded in 753 BCE, into the dominant power of the ancient Mediterranean world. Its history is usually divided into three phases: the Roman Kingdom (753–509 BCE), the Roman Republic (509–27 BCE), and the Roman Empire (27 BCE–476 CE in the West).

From Republic to Empire

The Republic was governed by elected magistrates, most importantly two annually elected consuls, and by the Senate, an advisory body of aristocrats. Rome expanded through conquest and alliance, defeating Carthage in the three Punic Wars (264–146 BCE) — during the second of which the Carthaginian general Hannibal famously crossed the Alps — and absorbing Greece, Egypt, Gaul, and much of the Near East. Political violence and civil wars marked the Republic's final century. Julius Caesar's crossing of the Rubicon in 49 BCE triggered civil war; after his assassination in 44 BCE, his adopted heir Octavian defeated Mark Antony and Cleopatra at the Battle of Actium in 31 BCE. In 27 BCE the Senate granted Octavian the title Augustus, marking the beginning of the Empire and of rule by emperors.

The Pax Romana and the height of empire

Augustus inaugurated the Pax Romana ("Roman Peace"), roughly 27 BCE to 180 CE, about two centuries of relative internal stability and prosperity. At its greatest extent under the emperor Trajan around 117 CE, the Empire spanned some 5 million square kilometers and ruled an estimated 50–60 million people — perhaps a fifth of humanity at the time. Its territory stretched from Britain to Egypt and from Spain to the Euphrates. A network of over 400,000 kilometers of roads (about 80,000 km paved) connected the provinces, giving rise to the saying "all roads lead to Rome."

Government, law, and society

The emperor held supreme military, judicial, and religious authority. Provinces were administered by governors and defended by the legions — professional soldiers who served around 25 years. Roman society was sharply stratified: patricians (aristocrats) and plebeians (commoners) among citizens, plus a large enslaved population that may have been a quarter to a third of Italy's inhabitants. Roman citizenship, initially exclusive, was extended to nearly all free inhabitants of the Empire by the Edict of Caracalla in 212 CE.

Rome's legal system is one of its most enduring legacies. Roman law introduced concepts such as legal contracts, property rights, and the principle that the accused is innocent until proven guilty; it was later codified in the Corpus Juris Civilis under the eastern emperor Justinian (529–534 CE) and forms the foundation of the civil-law tradition used across much of the world today.

Engineering and culture

Roman engineering achievements include aqueducts that carried water dozens of kilometers by gravity, the widespread use of concrete (opus caementicium), the arch and the dome, public baths, sewage systems, and monumental architecture such as the Colosseum (completed 80 CE, seating around 50,000) and the Pantheon, whose unreinforced concrete dome remains the largest of its kind. Latin, the Roman language, evolved into the Romance languages (Italian, French, Spanish, Portuguese, Romanian) and supplied much of English vocabulary.

Christianity and division

Christianity spread through the Empire despite periodic persecution. The emperor Constantine legalized it with the Edict of Milan in 313 CE and convened the Council of Nicaea in 325 CE. In 380 CE, under Theodosius I, Christianity became the state religion. In 285 CE Diocletian had divided the Empire administratively; in 330 CE Constantine founded a new eastern capital at Constantinople (modern Istanbul). The Empire was permanently split into Western and Eastern halves in 395 CE.

Decline and fall

The Western Roman Empire declined amid economic strain, political instability, overreliance on mercenaries, and pressure from migrating peoples (often called "barbarian" invasions). Rome was sacked by the Visigoths in 410 CE and by the Vandals in 455 CE. The conventional end date is 476 CE, when the Germanic leader Odoacer deposed the last Western emperor, Romulus Augustulus. The Eastern Roman Empire, known as the Byzantine Empire, endured for nearly another thousand years until Constantinople fell to the Ottoman Turks in 1453 CE. Rome's influence on law, language, government, engineering, and Western culture remains profound to this day.
