The French Revolution (1789–1799)

The French Revolution was a decade of radical political and social upheaval in France that overthrew the monarchy, restructured society, and reshaped politics across Europe and the wider world. It gave enduring currency to the ideals of liberty, equality, and fraternity and marked a decisive break from the old order of hereditary privilege.

Causes

Several pressures converged. France's society was legally divided into three "estates": the clergy (First Estate) and the nobility (Second Estate), who together were roughly 2% of the population but held vast privileges and paid little tax, and the commoners (Third Estate), who bore the tax burden and ranged from wealthy merchants to impoverished peasants. The monarchy under Louis XVI was nearly bankrupt, partly from costly wars including support for the American Revolution. Poor harvests in the late 1780s drove up bread prices, causing widespread hunger. Enlightenment thinkers such as Rousseau, Voltaire, and Montesquieu had spread ideas of popular sovereignty, natural rights, and the separation of powers, undermining the legitimacy of absolute monarchy.

Outbreak (1789)

Facing fiscal crisis, Louis XVI convened the Estates-General in May 1789 for the first time since 1614. Deadlocked over voting procedures that disadvantaged the Third Estate, its representatives broke away and declared themselves the National Assembly, vowing in the Tennis Court Oath (June 1789) not to disband until they had written a constitution. On 14 July 1789 a Parisian crowd stormed the Bastille, a royal fortress and prison that symbolized despotism — an event still commemorated as France's national day. In August the Assembly abolished feudal privileges and adopted the Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen, proclaiming that men are born free and equal in rights.

The constitutional monarchy and its collapse

From 1789 to 1792 France attempted a constitutional monarchy. Church lands were nationalized, and the Constitution of 1791 limited the king's powers. But the king's attempted flight in 1791 (halted at Varennes) destroyed trust in him, and war with Austria and Prussia began in April 1792. In August 1792 the monarchy was overthrown; in September the First Republic was proclaimed. Louis XVI was tried for treason and executed by guillotine in January 1793; his queen, Marie Antoinette, followed in October.

The Reign of Terror (1793–1794)

Beset by foreign invasion and internal revolt, the Republic came under the control of the Committee of Public Safety, dominated by Maximilien Robespierre and the radical Jacobins. During the Reign of Terror, revolutionary tribunals executed those deemed enemies of the Revolution. Estimates suggest around 17,000 people were officially executed and tens of thousands more died in prison or without trial. The guillotine became the era's grim emblem. The Terror ended when Robespierre himself was overthrown and executed in July 1794 (the Thermidorian Reaction).

The Directory and the rise of Napoleon

A more conservative government, the Directory, ruled from 1795 to 1799 amid corruption, economic trouble, and continued war. It was in this unstable period that a young and successful general, Napoleon Bonaparte, rose to prominence through victories in Italy and Egypt. In November 1799 (the coup of 18 Brumaire) Napoleon seized power, ending the Revolution and establishing the Consulate with himself as First Consul; he crowned himself Emperor in 1804.

Legacy

Though it descended into violence and gave way to Napoleon's empire, the French Revolution had far-reaching consequences. It abolished feudalism and legal privilege, established the principle that sovereignty rests with the nation and its citizens, and popularized concepts of constitutional government, secular authority, and universal rights. The Napoleonic Code (1804) that followed codified equality before the law and property rights and influenced legal systems across Europe and Latin America. The Revolution inspired later movements for democracy and national self-determination and remains a defining event in the emergence of the modern world. Its slogan — Liberté, Égalité, Fraternité — endures as the motto of the French Republic.
